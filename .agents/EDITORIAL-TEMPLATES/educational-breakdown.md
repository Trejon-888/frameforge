# Editorial Template: Educational Breakdown

**Intent:** Help the viewer understand a concept, process, or system. When the video ends, they should be able to explain it to someone else. The edit makes complex information feel accessible and organized.

**Overlay Count:** 7–10
**Pacing:** Content-aligned. Overlays follow the teaching structure, not a rhythm.
**Visual Density:** Medium. Visual events at every new concept or step, breathing room within each section.
**Platform Fit:** YouTube (primary), LinkedIn articles, course content, webinar recordings

---

## Why This Template Works

Educational content has a cognitive contract with the viewer: "I will teach you something if you follow along." The edit's job is to make following along easy.

The brain retains information better when it arrives through multiple channels simultaneously. Hearing "there are 3 steps" while seeing a visual of 3 steps creates double encoding. The edit doesn't add information — it reinforces and organizes the information already being delivered.

The most important editing decision in this template is **structure signaling**. When the viewer can see that they're at "Step 2 of 3," they relax and absorb. When the structure is invisible, they use cognitive bandwidth tracking where they are instead of learning.

---

## Edit Architecture

### Structural Principle: Follow the Teaching Logic

Unlike authority-builder or viral-hook, the overlay sequence here should mirror the speaker's actual structure:
- If they present 3 steps, you show 3 chapter markers
- If they give a before/after example, you show a comparison card
- If they cite a statistic as proof, you show that number
- If they name a specific tool or concept, you may highlight it

Do not add overlays just to fill time. Do not add overlays during examples or stories — let those breathe.

### Act 1: Establish Context (0 – 10% of video)

**Overlay 1: Topic Framing (first 5 seconds)**
*Technique: kinetic-phrase OR chapter-marker*
Tell the viewer what they're about to learn. This creates the mental "folder" they'll file new information into. If the speaker opens with a question ("Have you ever tried to...?"), reinforce it visually.
- Position: headline zone (top-center)
- Duration: 4000–5000ms

**Overlay 2: Identity / Credential (optional, 4–10s)**
*Technique: lower-third-identity*
For educational content, authority matters more than in entertainment. Include the speaker's identity if they have a relevant credential ("Built 3 SaaS companies", "Former Google Engineer", "Agency owner, 8 years").
- Position: lower-third
- Duration: 5000ms
- Only include if credential is specific and relevant to the topic

### Act 2: Structure + Teaching (10% – 80% of video)

**Chapter Markers: at every major section transition**
*Technique: chapter-marker (thin line + label)*
When the speaker transitions between major sections, a chapter marker signals the shift. This is particularly valuable for longer content (5+ minutes) where the viewer may re-watch.
- Position: top of frame (y: 80–120px) or lower-third zone
- Duration: 3500–4000ms
- Timing: at the natural pause between sections

**Concept Highlights: at specialized vocabulary or named concepts**
*Technique: social-proof-band OR word-slam (lighter version)*
When the speaker introduces a specific term, framework, or named concept they'll reference repeatedly, a brief visual highlight embeds it. "The AIDA framework" → AIDA appears visually as they say it.
- Position: headline zone
- Duration: 3000–4000ms (shorter — this is reinforcement, not the main event)

**Process/Step Visualization: when describing sequences**
*Technique: process-flow OR numbered list*
If the speaker says "there are 3 steps" or "first... then... finally...", visualize the sequence. Show all steps but highlight the current one as the speaker moves through them.
- Position: center-frame (full-width)
- Duration: matches the length of the process explanation
- Build: animate each step as the speaker reaches it

**Proof Points: at every specific number or result cited**
*Technique: number-reveal OR data-progress*
Every quantitative claim needs a visual. Not for virality — for credibility. "A 47% completion rate" is remembered better when you see 47%.
- Position: stat zone or right-anchored card
- Duration: 5000–6000ms

### Act 3: Summary + Next Step (80% – 100% of video)

**Summary Recap (optional — for longer videos)**
*Technique: process-flow (full list, all steps visible)*
If the video covered 3+ distinct steps/sections, a brief recap visual at the end provides closure and gives skippers something to land on.
- Position: center-frame
- Duration: 5000–6000ms (fast reveal of all items)

**CTA with Value Continuation**
*Technique: cta-card*
The educational CTA should promise more of what was just delivered: "More like this every week →" or "Full guide in bio" or "Course link in description". Connect the action to the value.
- Duration: 7000–9000ms

## Special Patterns

**The "Before/After" moment:**
When the speaker describes a transformation (how they used to do X vs. how they do it now), use a comparison-card overlay:
```
[BEFORE: 3 hours manually] → [AFTER: 12 minutes automated]
```
This is one of the highest-retention visual formats for educational content.

**The "Named Framework" moment:**
When the speaker names their system, framework, or method, make the name fill the frame for 2-3 seconds. This is the moment they want the viewer to remember and search for.

**The "Number in Context" moment:**
Not just "47%" but "47% of businesses fail to do this" — show the number AND a 3-word context label below it. The context makes the number stick.

---

## Timing Formula

For an 8-minute educational video (adapt proportionally):
```
0s        → Topic framing / hook
15s       → Identity/credential
1:30      → Chapter marker: "PART 1"
2:45      → First key concept highlight
4:00      → Process visualization (steps 1-3)
5:20      → Chapter marker: "PART 2"
6:10      → First proof/stat
7:00      → Summary recap
7:20      → CTA card
```

For a 90-second educational clip: 5-6 overlays, no chapter markers, focus on 1 key concept + 1 stat + CTA.

---

## What NOT To Do

**Don't caption what the speaker is already saying.**
If the speaker says "Step 1 is understanding your market," do NOT create a lower-third that says "Step 1: Understand your market." That is transcription, not editing. The overlay should add what's *not being said verbally* — the framework label, the visual representation, the data.

**Don't add overlays during stories or examples.**
When the speaker tells a client story or gives an example, the overlay system should go quiet. Stories work through immersion. Visual noise breaks the immersion.

**Don't rush chapter markers.**
A chapter marker that disappears before the viewer reads it is worse than no chapter marker. 3500ms minimum.

---

## Quality Signals

**This template is working when:**
- A viewer who skimmed (skipped ahead 3 times) can still understand the structure
- The visual system makes the video feel "organized" rather than "polished"
- Every overlay adds information that supplements what's being said, not restates it
- The viewer leaves with 1-2 clear, named concepts they can use

**This template is failing when:**
- Overlays appear during examples/stories (should be empty)
- Identical techniques are used consecutively
- No chapter markers despite the video having distinct sections
- The CTA doesn't continue the value of the video content

## Style Kit Recommendations

**Primary:** `clean-professional` — clarity and organization match this template's intent
**Alternative:** `kinetic-orange` — for tech/AI educational content, adds energy
**Avoid:** `bold-dark-social` — the urgency and density are the wrong signals for educational content
