# Editorial Template: Social Proof Stack

**Intent:** Build undeniable credibility through layered evidence. The viewer should finish the video feeling that dismissing this person or product would require ignoring an overwhelming stack of proof.

**Overlay Count:** 8–12
**Pacing:** Rhythmic and building. Each proof point adds weight. The pace accelerates slightly as evidence accumulates.
**Visual Density:** Medium-high. Evidence-dense but not chaotic.
**Platform Fit:** LinkedIn, YouTube, Instagram (testimonial/results content), Twitter/X case study threads

---

## Why This Template Works

Social proof operates on a simple psychological principle: the more evidence accumulates, the harder it becomes to dismiss. One testimonial is anecdotal. Three numbers are coincidence. Ten specific claims across different dimensions feel overwhelming and undeniable.

The key is specificity. Vague social proof ("clients love us", "great results") has zero impact. Specific social proof ("237 agencies, $4B managed, 92% retention, 0 manual hours") is impossible to argue with. Every overlay in this template should be specific.

The building rhythm matters. Each overlay doesn't stand alone — it adds to a mounting case. By the CTA, the viewer should feel like they would be making an objectively bad decision not to act.

## Edit Architecture

### The Proof Stack Principle

Every overlay in acts 1 and 2 should answer one of these questions with a specific, verifiable-sounding answer:
1. **Scale**: How many? How big? How long? ("237 agencies", "$4B managed", "5 years")
2. **Speed**: How fast? How long did it take? ("12 minutes vs 3 hours", "first client in 48 hours")
3. **Reliability**: What percentage? What rate? ("97% retention", "23 of 23 campaigns")
4. **Names**: Who? (named clients/companies, even anonymized, add more weight than abstractions)
5. **Transformation**: Before vs. after with specific numbers

### Act 1: Establish the Stakes (0 – 15% of video)

**Overlay 1: Opening Claim / Pattern Interrupt (0–3s)**
*Technique: word-slam OR kinetic-phrase*
The opening line of this video type is usually a bold, surprising, or counter-intuitive claim. Make it visual immediately.
- Position: headline zone or full-center
- Duration: 3500–4500ms

**Overlay 2: Identity (credentialed) (4–10s)**
*Technique: lower-third-identity (with result)*
Unlike a standard identity overlay, in this template the lower-third should include a result alongside the identity: "ENRIQUE · Built 5 AI agencies · $4M ARR". The credential IS the first proof point.
- Position: lower-third zone
- Duration: 5000–6000ms

### Act 2: The Proof Stack (15% – 80% of video)

This is the core of the template. Each overlay is one layer of proof. Techniques should vary — don't repeat the same type twice in a row.

**The Six Proof Layer Types (mix and match based on what the transcript provides):**

**Scale proof:**
*Technique: number-reveal (large hero count)*
A big number — agencies served, revenue generated, clients acquired, hours saved. Center-frame, large typography, count-up animation.

**Speed proof:**
*Technique: comparison-card OR data-progress*
Before/after timeline. "3 hours → 12 minutes". Or a progress bar showing compression of time/effort.

**Reliability proof:**
*Technique: number-reveal (percentage)*
A percentage stat that implies systematic reliability: "97% of clients renew". The percentage format implies a track record, not a single instance.

**Social proof (named or typed):**
*Technique: credential-card OR social-proof-band*
A specific client type, industry, or company name with their result. "Fortune 500 companies" or "Real estate agencies in 23 states" is more convincing than "many clients."

**Transformation proof:**
*Technique: comparison-card OR two-column layout*
The clearest before/after in the video. Find the moment where the speaker describes the biggest gap between the old way and the new way.

**Authority proof:**
*Technique: quote-pull*
A specific, quotable line from the speaker that encapsulates the value proposition. Large, beautiful, held.

**Layer building guidance:**
- Alternate between number-heavy and text-heavy overlays (cognitive variety)
- Lead with the most surprising/unexpected proof early, build to the most impressive later
- Leave 5-7 seconds between each overlay — this is the "let it sink in" space
- The peak proof (biggest, most impressive number) should appear at 60-70% of video duration, not at the end

### Act 3: The Inevitable CTA (80% – 100% of video)

After the proof stack, the CTA should feel like a logical conclusion, not a sales pitch.

**Outro Energy (optional):**
*Technique: canvas-animation*
A brief atmospheric visual — 8-10 seconds — that shifts the energy from "evidence delivery" to "invitation". Orbital rings, particle system, smooth waveform. Creates a psychological beat before the ask.

**The Ask (final 15%):**
*Technique: cta-card (specific value)*
The CTA must be specific to the proof just delivered: not just "follow me" but "follow to see how we do this". Not just "book a call" but "book a call to see this for your agency". The proof created desire — the CTA channels it.
- Duration: 8000–10000ms (longer than other templates — the decision is warmer)

## Timing Formula

For a 60-second testimonial/results video:
```
0.5s     → Pattern interrupt / opening claim
4s       → Identity (with built-in credential)
[empty — let speaker build stakes]
14s      → Scale proof (number)
22s      → Speed proof (comparison)
30s      → Reliability proof (percentage)
[empty — breathing room before peak]
38s      → Hero proof (biggest/most impressive)
44s      → Authority proof (quote pull)
50s      → Outro canvas animation
55s      → CTA card (specific ask)
```

For a 90-second results video, add 2 more proof layers at ~25s and ~60s.

## Calibrating the Stack

**Too little proof:** The video feels like claims without evidence. Overlays are mostly text decorations.
- Solution: Find more specific numbers. "Many clients" → look for an exact count. "Good results" → look for a specific metric.

**Too much proof:** The stack feels like a desperate sales pitch. Viewer becomes skeptical.
- Solution: Cut the 3 weakest proof overlays. A stack of 5 specific, undeniable proofs > a stack of 10 vague ones.

**Wrong order:** Strongest proof appears too early; later proof feels anticlimactic.
- Solution: Build. Start with the base (scale/count), move through mid-tier (specific results), peak at the most impressive/surprising number.

---

## Specificity Rules

**Always prefer:**
- "$4,237/month" over "$4K/month"
- "23 agencies in Q1" over "many agencies"
- "3 hours → 11 minutes" over "much faster"
- "97.3% retention" over "high retention"

**The specificity paradox:** The more specific the number, the more credible it feels, even if a rounded number would be equally true. "47 clients" feels more real than "50 clients."

---

## Quality Signals

**This template is working when:**
- A skeptical viewer finds themselves thinking "okay, but also..." before each new piece of evidence arrives
- Every overlay cites something specific, not vague
- The proof layers build on each other rather than repeating the same type of claim
- The CTA connects directly to the specific evidence just shown

**This template is failing when:**
- Proof overlays use vague language ("great results", "many clients")
- The same technique is used 3+ times consecutively
- There are too many overlays (>12) — dilutes each individual proof point
- The biggest number is revealed too early (nothing to build toward)
